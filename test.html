Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs) Information

HIV/AIDS

Transmission

  • Through blood, semen, vaginal fluids, rectal fluids, and breast milk.
  • Commonly spread through unprotected sex, sharing needles, and from mother to child during childbirth or breastfeeding.
  • Less commonly, through blood transfusions and organ transplants with infected blood.

Symptoms

  • Early stage: Flu-like symptoms such as fever, sore throat, and fatigue (acute HIV infection).
  • Chronic stage: Asymptomatic period where the virus is still active but reproduces at very low levels.
  • Advanced stage (AIDS): Severe immune system damage, weight loss, chronic diarrhea, night sweats, and opportunistic infections like tuberculosis and certain cancers.

Treatment

  • Antiretroviral therapy (ART) to manage the virus, keep the immune system functioning, and reduce the viral load to undetectable levels.
  • Regular medical check-ups and blood tests to monitor health and treatment effectiveness.

Chlamydia

Transmission

  • Through vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
  • Can be transmitted even if the infected partner does not ejaculate.
  • Can be passed from mother to baby during childbirth, causing eye infections or pneumonia in the newborn.

Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic, especially in women.
  • Genital pain, discharge, burning sensation during urination.
  • Women may experience abdominal pain, bleeding between periods, and pain during intercourse.
  • Men may experience pain and swelling in one or both testicles (though this is less common).

Treatment

  • Antibiotics such as azithromycin or doxycycline.
  • Both sexual partners should be treated to prevent re-infection.
  • Avoid sexual activity until treatment is completed and follow-up tests confirm the infection is cleared.

Gonorrhea

Transmission

  • Through vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
  • Can be transmitted even without ejaculation.
  • Can be passed from mother to baby during childbirth, leading to serious health problems for the newborn.

Symptoms

  • Painful urination, abnormal discharge, genital itching.
  • Women may experience increased vaginal discharge, vaginal bleeding between periods, and pelvic pain.
  • Men may experience testicular pain and swollen lymph nodes in the groin.
  • Rectal infections can cause discharge, anal itching, soreness, bleeding, or painful bowel movements.
  • Throat infections may cause sore throat or swollen lymph nodes.

Treatment

  • Antibiotics such as ceftriaxone, often combined with azithromycin or doxycycline to cover potential co-infection with chlamydia.
  • Both sexual partners should be treated to prevent re-infection.
  • Avoid sexual activity until treatment is completed and follow-up tests confirm the infection is cleared.

Syphilis

Transmission

  • Through direct contact with a syphilis sore during vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
  • Can be transmitted from mother to child during pregnancy or childbirth.

Symptoms

  • Primary stage: Painless sore(s) at the infection site (genitals, anus, or mouth).
  • Secondary stage: Skin rashes, mucous membrane lesions, fever, swollen lymph nodes, sore throat, hair loss, headaches, weight loss, muscle aches, and fatigue.
  • Latent stage: No symptoms, but the infection remains in the body.
  • Tertiary stage: Severe medical problems affecting the heart, brain, and other organs, occurring years after the initial infection.

Treatment

  • Antibiotics, typically penicillin.
  • Regular follow-up blood tests to ensure the infection is cured.

Herpes (HSV-1 and HSV-2)

Transmission

  • Through vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
  • HSV-1 is typically spread through oral contact but can cause genital herpes.
  • HSV-2 is typically spread through sexual contact and causes genital herpes.

Symptoms

  • Painful blisters or sores on the genitals, rectum, or mouth.
  • Initial outbreak can be severe, including flu-like symptoms.
  • Recurrent outbreaks are common but usually less severe.

Treatment

  • Antiviral medications like acyclovir, famciclovir, and valacyclovir to reduce symptoms and frequency of outbreaks.
  • No cure, but medication can help manage symptoms.

Human Papillomavirus (HPV)

Transmission

  • Through vaginal, anal, or oral sex.
  • Skin-to-skin contact with an infected area.

Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic.
  • Some strains cause genital warts.
  • High-risk strains can lead to cancers (cervical, anal, throat).

Treatment

  • No cure, but the immune system often clears the infection naturally.
  • Treatment for genital warts includes topical medications, cryotherapy, or surgical removal.
  • Regular screening (Pap smears) and HPV vaccination can prevent complications.
